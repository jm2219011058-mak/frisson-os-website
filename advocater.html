The Mask comes off
January 2027

In the early 20th century, the Wright brothers set out on the journey to build the first controllable aircraft. Half a century later, NASA embarked on the mission to put humans on the moon. Combustion, penicillin, nuclear fission; the discovery of the Americas, the drafting of the Constitution, the Normandy landings—history is written by heroic figures in pursuit of what is right.

What is your mission?

We often think of our life's purpose as a mission. Your mission might be to reach a specific milestone: to become a senator, to publish a book, to make a million dollars. But not every mission has an endpoint. You might be working to maximize your net worth, or to lift more weight in the gym than anyone else. Maybe you simply want to enjoy life to the fullest before you die. That's a far cry from the refrigerator or running water, but it's still a good life.

As you may already have realized, not all missions are created equal.

Put simply: a good adventure makes the future better than the world we have today, while a bad adventure does nothing to improve the world at all—or even makes it worse.

Today, we find ourselves in a crisis.

Silicon Valley's elite—the top operators, the founders with successful exits, and the most influential investors—are almost all walking down the wrong path. After exiting your first startup, turning to venture capital to fight your peers for a share of the hot deals is in itself the wrong choice. Pontificating about philosophy on Twitter is also the wrong choice. Retiring at 35 to lounge on a yacht and answer emails is an even more egregiously wrong choice. Even among the exceptional talents who do choose to build companies, most opt for safe, incremental bets—another SaaS company, another point-and-click consumer startup, another digital version of the Beanie Baby. Pursuits like these not only fail to push the world forward; they squander the opportunity. Humanity faces many important challenges, some of them matters of life and death, and yet they go unaddressed.

In other words, if you are a person of extraordinary talent, giving up the pursuit of a noble cause is not a trivial matter—it is a loss for humanity.
